Robert E. Lee

Career Soldier

 

Marble Man

Pre 1861: Robert E. Lee was a career soldier and Southern aristocratic who despised slavery and secession.

 

But his loyalty to his family and home state of Virginia caused him to renounce command of the Union army and cast his lot with the Confederacy. Lee and the Army of Northern Virginia grew to prominence early in the war and at times appeared invincible. Lee was a master tactician and strategist who earned the love and loyalty of his soldiers and has been described, in the words of historian David McCullough, as “the most beloved general in American history.”

 

Robert Edward Lee, born in 1807, was the son of legendary Revolutionary War hero Henry “Light Horse Harry” Lee. Young Robert’s childhood was marred by his father’s absence and the scandal of indebtedness he left behind.

 

But he went on to enroll in the United States Military Academy at West Point, graduated with the Class of 1829 and drew an assignment to the Corps of Engineers. Lee spent the early part of his military career supervising the construction of fortifications as well as learning the intricacies of being a husband and father.

 

During his service in the Mexican War of 1846-1847, Lee became a member of the staff of General Winfield Scott and undertook a number of dangerous reconnaissance missions that helped in the capture of Mexico City.

 

It was during the war with Mexico that Lee became familiar with many soldiers who would become both his subordinates and enemies during the Civil War. In 1852 Lee returned to West Point assuming the position of superintendent. In 1855 Lee returned to the field as the commanding officer of the 2nd U.S. Cavalry in Texas.

1861

Lee's Refusal

1861

 

"I shall never bear arms against the Union, but it may be necessary for me to carry a musket in the defense of my native state, Virginia, in which case I shall not prove recreant to my duty."

— Robert E. Lee
 

April 18th: In 1859, by now holding the rank of colonel, Lee became involved in the national turmoil that would lead to civil war.

 

In October of that year Lee took command of the troops sent to quell the uprising started by John Brown at Harpers Ferry. Ever the disciplined soldier Lee strove to rise above the political difficulties that the times engendered. It was hard even for the “Marble Man” to avoid. In April 1861 he chose to turn down Lincoln’s offer of command of the Union army and returned to his home state.

 

With all these reasons pointing to why he would stay with the North, why did he decide to side with the South? It came down to his view of state sovereignty. Lee viewed himself as a Virginian more than an American. He would follow the choice of his state even though he personally disagreed with it.

1862

The Bloodiest Day

1862

 

"It is well that war is so terrible, otherwise we should grow too fond of it."

— Robert E. Lee
 

September 17th: Lee was commissioned a major general in the armed forces of the state of Virginia and after unheralded command in western Virginia became a member of the military staff of Confederate President Jefferson Davis.

 

With the wounding of General Joseph E. Johnston, Davis promoted Lee to the command of the Army of Northern Virginia in June 1862. Lee’s maneuvering in the Seven Days Battle that month was brilliant and drove “those people”, as Lee liked to call his Northern opponents, away from Richmond. In the fall of 1862 Lee mounted an invasion of the North that resulted in the bloodiest day in American history, the Battle of Antietam.

 

The Army of the Potomac, under the command of George McClellan, mounted a series of powerful assaults against Robert E. Lee’s forces near Sharpsburg, Maryland, on September 17, 1862. The morning assault and vicious Confederate counterattacks swept back and forth through Miller’s Cornfield and the West Woods. Later, towards the center of the battlefield, Union assaults against the Sunken Road pierced the Confederate center after a terrible struggle.

 

Late in the day, the third and final major assault by the Union army pushed over a bullet-strewn stone bridge at Antietam Creek. Just as the Federal forces began to collapse the Confederate right, the timely arrival of A.P. Hill’s division from Harpers Ferry helped to drive the Army of the Potomac back once more.

 

The bloodiest single day in American military history ended in a draw, but the Confederate retreat gave Abraham Lincoln the “victory” he desired before issuing the Emancipation Proclamation.

Joseph E. Johnston

1863

Lee's Greatest Defeat

1863

July: Taking advantage of the ineptitude of Union field commanders and desirous of drawing the war away from his beloved Virginia, Lee returned to a more ambitious plan of invading the North in the spring of 1863.

 

Despite the loss of “Stonewall” Jackson at the Battle of Chancellorsville in May Lee once more put his reorganized army on the march north in the hope of threatening the Union government in such a way that peace would be the only recourse.

 

At Gettysburg on July 1-3, 1863, Union forces under the command of General George Gordon Meade decisively defeated Lee and his army. Not only did the army suffer on the battlefield but also the aura of Lee’s invincibility was now in question.

 

Lee’s health was also in decline as he may have been suffering from a form of heart disease as well as the ramifications from a fall he suffered. Bickering and the lack of cooperation on the part of some of his subordinates also contributed to the defeat of the Army of Northern Virginia. As the army retreated back into Virginia Lee was resigned to take no more offensives against the North.

1865

The Downfall

1865

The spring of 1864 brought a new nemesis to confront Lee in the person of Ulysses S. Grant who had been appointed general in charge of all Union forces by President Lincoln.

 

It was Grant’s bulldog persistence in the Overland Campaign and in front of Petersburg that slowly brought the war to an end. But that’s not to say that Lee didn’t counter every move Grant made in desperately trying to stave off the inevitable. The industrial capability of the North, ability to provide re-enforcements, extensive means of supply and victory in other theaters of operation were just some of the other factors in the Confederacy’s defeat.

 

Just a year later, in the spring of 1865 Grant trapped Lee as he and the army attempted to flee into North Carolina following the fall of Richmond. On April 9, 1865 Lee and Grant met at Appomattox Court House and concluded the surrender of the Army of Northern Virginia.

Washington & Lee

Post War: Following the surrender Lee returned to his Richmond home and in August became the president of little Washington College in Lexington, Virginia.

 

Lee’s life after the war was plagued by deteriorating health but he consented to join his daughter Agnes on a tour of the South in 1870. There he renewed acquaintances and friendships with many of his former soldiers as well as receiving numerous honors and testimonials.

 

In September a weary Lee suffered a debilitating stroke after attending a prayer meeting. Lee never recovered and died on October 14, 1870. He was buried in a crypt on the Washington College campus. The college was soon renamed “Washington & Lee College.”
