The Life of An Immigrant

Pre War: Major Hugo Hildebrandt was a foreign-born soldier who rose through the ranks to command a regiment in the Union Army at the Battle of Gettysburg.

 

Hildebrandt was born in Hungary in 1832, was educated at military school and took part in the failed Hungarian uprising of 1848. He came to the United States in the early 1850s along with other Hungarian refugees and took a job with the United States Coastal Survey. But he soon returned to Europe and fought with Giuseppe Garibaldi in his campaign to unite Italy. He was with Garibaldi when he triumphantly entered Rome in 1860.

1861

Entering War

1861

July: He returned to the United States in the winter of 1860 and enlisted as a Sergeant Major in the 39th New York Volunteer Infantry Regiment, the “Garibaldi Guard.”

 

The regiment enlisted mainly immigrants and boosted companies composed of Hungarians, Italians, Germans, Swiss, French, and Spaniards. Orders were sometimes given in 7 different languages and the regiment became known as “Lincoln’s Foreign Legion." Hildebrandt and the regiment moved into the defenses of Washington. D.C. and took part in the advance on Bull Run in July.

1862

Rising Ranks

1862

June: Mismanagement and ineptitude on the part of the command staff allowed the capable Hildebrandt to advance upwards through the ranks.

 

By June 1862 he wore the shoulder straps of Major. In the fall of 1862 he took part in the Harpers Ferry campaign where the regiment, under his command, fought valiantly but was forced to surrender. The regiment was eventually paroled and returned to the fortifications surrounding Washington.

The Straps of a Major General

1863

Taking Charge

1863

June: Once again questions surrounding the conduct of the regiment’s commanding officer, Frederick George D’Utassy, impacted Major Hildebrandt.

 

D’Utassy’s ouster by a court martial conviction caused the command of the regiment to fall squarely on the shoulders of Hildebrandt, this coming at a time when the Army of the Potomac was marching in pursuit of Lee’s Army of Northern Virginia as it invaded Pennsylvania in June of 1863.

 

Hildebrandt’s appointment by Brigadier General Hayes was rewarded by the regiment’s performance during the battles on the second and third day at Gettysburg. The second days saw Hildebrandt leading a charge that recaptured some of the guns lost by the 5th U.S. Artillery while on the third day the regiment helped repulse Pickett’s Charge. It was during this part of the engagement where Hildebrandt was seriously wounded.

Frederick G. Utassy (right) & his brother

Life After

After his recuperation Hildebrandt returned to his command but a respiratory illness contracted in the fall of 1863 forced him to resign his commission.

 

He was later given a commission in the Veteran Reserve Corps where he served for the remainder of the war. In the years that followed, despite worsening health, Hildebrandt worked for the Freedmen’s Bureau in North Carolina and was rewarded for his service by President Grant with an appointment as U.S. Consul to Crete. There, he married the daughter of the Austrian consul.

 

Following the closing of the consulate, Hildebrandt returned to the United States and took up residence in Brooklyn, New York and was employed for a time by U.S. Customs Service as a translator. His deteriorating health was diagnosed as pulmonary tuberculosis and he succumbed to the disease in April 1896 at age 64.
