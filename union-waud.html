Alfred Waud

War Correspondent

 

On The Front Lines

The British born Alfred Waud is among a group of skilled artists such as Theodore R. Davis, Edwin Forbes, Winslow Homer, and Henry Wake who put their lives in danger in order to record events of the Civil War through pen and ink sketches.

 

Before emigration, Alfred Waud had entered the Government School of Design at Somerset House, London, with the intention of becoming a marine painter. This did not come to fruition, but as a student, he also worked as a painter of theatrical scenery.

 

He intended to pursue that work in the United States, when he immigrated in 1850, seeking employment with actor and playwright John Brougham. In the 1850s, he worked variously as an illustrator for a Boston periodical, the Carpet-Bag, and provided illustrations for books such as Hunter's Panoramic Guide from Niagara to Quebec (1857).

 

Waud continued to be a prolific illustrator, doing numerous illustrations for Harper's Weekly and other prominent publications, achieving his greatest fame in his post-War work. Waud died in 1891 in Marietta, Georgia, while touring battlefields of the South.

Thanksgiving

1861: This early drawing shows union officers enjoying a Thanksgiving meal, a Holiday created by President Lincoln.

Rowdy Drunkards

1862: Here, Waud depicts soldiers being reprimanded for excessive drunkenness.

Comm Lines

1862: Two soldiers carry a telegraph during the battle of Fredericksburg. The telegraph was the main form of communication during the war. Therefore, the delivery of communications was paramount to both sides.

Camp Wedding

1863: This sketch depicts the Wedding of NJ Captain Uriah De Hart & Nellie Lammond at a service held in their camp.

Process

1864: The technology to convert Waud's drawings to photographs for reproduction did not exist. Therefore, he would mail in his work to Harper's Weekly and they would have a woodcut artist reinterpret his original drawing for reproduction in the magazine.
