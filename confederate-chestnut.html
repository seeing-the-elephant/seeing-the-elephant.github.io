Mary Chestnut

 

Confederate Insider

Mary Boykin Chestnut was the wife of a Southern politician and diarist who left behind what has become a treasure trove of insight into the workings and thoughts of the Confederacy during the Civil War.

 

Characterized as “sharp-eyed” and “sharp-tongued”, Mary Chestnut began keeping a journal in February 1861 in which she wrote regularly during the war. Only 7 volumes of this journal remain as some were lost, some destroyed or in the opinion of some historians, never written. What do survive are entries from 1861, parts of 1862 and 1865.

 

Mary Chesnut was born on March 31, 1823, on her maternal grandparents' plantation, called Mount Pleasant, near Stateburg, South Carolina, in the High Hills of Santee. Her parents were Mary Boykin (1804–85) and Stephen Decatur Miller (1788–1838), who had served as a U.S. Representative.

In 1829 he was elected governor of South Carolina and in 1831 as a U.S. Senator. The family then lived in Charleston. Mary was the oldest of four children; she had a younger brother Stephen and two sisters: Catherine and Sarah Amelia.

 

At age 17, Miller began her formal education in Charleston, where she boarded at Mme. Talvande's French School for Young Ladies, which attracted daughters from the elite of the planter class. Talvande was among the many French colonial refugees who had settled in Charleston from Saint-Domingue (Haiti) after its Revolution. Miller became fluent in French and German, and received a strong education.

 

Leaving politics, her father took his family to Mississippi where he bought extensive acreage. It was a crude, rough frontier compared to Charleston. He owned three cotton plantations and hundreds of slaves. Mary lived in Mississippi for short periods between school terms but was much more fond of the city.

 

1861

Rocky Start

1861

 

“There is a perfect magazine of discord and discontent in that cabinet. It only wants to apply a torch and up they go.”

— Mary Chestnut
 

Chestnut was married to James Chestnut, a former U.S. Senator from South Carolina and one of the largest slave owners in the state.

 

At the beginning of the war he served as an aide to General P.G.T. Beauregard and later returned to Richmond to serve on the staff of President Jefferson Davis. Her husband’s position put Mary in a place where she was privy to “wartime gossip, insights and characterizations about life in high Confederate society.”

Surprisingly enough the only thing she hated more than slavery, she called it a “monstrous system”, were “Yankees.” She offers biting criticism and well as a feminist point of view on several subjects.

Mary & James Chestnut

Fort Sumter

April 12th: During the attack on Fort Sumter, Mary Chestnut fearfully yet excitedly awaited the conclusion of the battle. Unable to do anymore than drink tea, she noted: “Not by one word or look can we detect any change in the demeanor of [the] negro servants…. They make no sign. Are they stolidly stupid or wiser than we are, silent and strong, biding their time?”

1862

Thrill of War

1862

 

“Crowded with soldiers. These new ones are running in, fairly. They fear the war will be over before they get a sight of the fun. Every man from every little country precinct wants a place in the picture ”

— Mary Chestnut
 

The quote above comes from Mary Chestnut's comment on soldiers in Charleston.

 

She saw such an eagerness for these men to join the battle. These young men, much like the South itself, saw a romanticism in war and foresaw a swift victory against the North. They couldn't allow their way of life to be trampled and destroyed by these "Yankees" and were excited and jubilant to start the battle.

1863

Freedom

1863

Mary never lost her distaste of slavery.

 

As a child on her paternal grandparents’ plantation, she had rescued a slave there from illiteracy with her grandmother’s consent. It was an establishment where slaves were treated humanely. She again broke the law at Mulberry, also with family permission, by teaching other slaves to read and write.

 

She commenced her diary as the South began to understand how formidable the forces were that were mustering to crush the Confederacy. James may have disliked slavery, but he was an ardent secessionist, serving on Gen. P.G.T. Beauregard’s staff, initially as a brigadier general and ultimately as a general. Although he saw action at First Manassas, his were primarily administrative duties.

“If anything can reconcile me to the idea of a horrid failure after all efforts to make good our independence of Yankees, it is Lincoln's proclamation freeing the negroes.”

— Mary Chestnut

1865

Deep Waters

1865

 

“It’s like a Greek tragedy, where you know what the outcome is bound to be. We’re living a Greek tragedy.”

— Mary Chestnut
 

April 7th: "Richmond has fallen and I have no heart to write about it."

 

But the time had come for General Lee to consider just such a necessity. He had been able to hold back the Union forces for almost 10 months at Petersburg until his depleted forces were worn out and his supplies dwindled to nothing. Finally, he came to believe that he could best serve the Confederate cause by abandoning its capital. Furgurson records that Lee asked Lt. Gen. John B. Gordon for his opinion as to the Confederate Army's next steps.

 

Gordon advised that the Confederacy should seek peace terms. If the terms were not acceptable, Gordon argued, the army should leave Richmond and Petersburg and retreat south to join Gen. Joseph E. Johnston's army in the Carolinas where their combined forces could concentrate on defeating the Union army under General William T. Sherman.

"Grant broke through our lines and Sherman cut through them. Stoneman is this side of Danville. They are too many for us. Everything is lost in Richmond, even our archives. Blue black is our horizon. Hood says we shall all be obliged to go West - to Texas, I mean, for our own part of the country will be overrun."

— Mary Chestnut

The Aftermath

 

“I daily part with my raiment for food. We find no one who will exchange eatables for Confederate money. So we are devouring our clothes.”

— Mary Chestnut
 

Following the war, which brought her family nothing but ruin, Mary made a serious effort to prepare her journals for publication which she hoped would help restore her family’s fortune.

 

Their Plantation near Mulberry, South Carolina was gutted by Union soldiers. Mary managed to make money selling butter and eggs with the help of her maid and she continued to write novels, translations and memoirs. Sidetracked by other literary efforts she returned to the journals in the 1880s but died before completing her work.

 

Several “tainted” versions made their way into print but it wasn’t until C. Vann Woodward’s Pulitzer Prize winning edition (1982), based on the 1880s revisions, did readers get an uncommon view into the inner working of the Confederate government

 

By his father's will, James Chesnut, Jr. had the use of Mulberry and Sandy Hill plantations only during his lifetime. In February 1885, both his and Mary's mother died. The plantations passed on to a male Chesnut descendant, and Mary Boykin Chesnut received almost no income for her support.

 

She also found her husband had many debts related to the estate which he had been unable to clear. She struggled in her last year, dying in 1886 at her home, Sarsfield, in Camden, South Carolina. She was buried next to her husband in Knights Hill Cemetery in Camden, South Carolina.
