Jefferson Davis

1st President of The Confederate States

 

South's Hero

Pre 1861: Jefferson Davis (1808-1889) was a Mexican War hero, U.S. senator from Mississippi, U.S. secretary of war and president of the Confederate States of America for the duration of the American Civil War (1861-1865).

 

Prior to the start of the war, Davis had argued against secession, but when Mississippi seceded he resigned from the U.S. Senate. In February 1861 he was elected president of the Confederacy. The outbreak of the Mexican War saw him rush off to volunteer his services and he became the colonel of the 1st Mississippi Rifles.

 

Covering himself with glory he returned a hero and was appointed Secretary of War under President Franklin Pierce. Under his administration the army grew in size and stature, as did Davis’ reputation. In 1857 he returned to the Senate and served on the military affairs committee. His politics were moderate and he was considered a champion of slavery and states’ rights.

1861

The Battles Begin

1861

April 12th, 1861: Davis, against the advice of Secretary of State Toombs, gave the order for his men to fire on Fort Sumter

 

1862

Davis's Best Move

1862

1862: With the secession of Southern states Davis found himself elected provisional President of the Confederate States of America in February 1861.

 

He considered a post as a field commander better suited to his abilities. But supporters called him “honest, pure, patriotic” and Davis surrounded himself with some able men. Quite possibly the best decision he ever made was the appointment of his military aide Robert E. Lee to command Confederate forces in 1862.

 

When General Joseph E. Johnston was wounded at the Battle of Seven Pines on May 31, Lee took command of what became the Army of Northern Virginia. He successfully repulsed the efforts of Union general George McClellan in the Peninsular campaign, concluding with the Battles of Seven Days: Oak Grove, Mechanicsville, Gaine’s Mill, Garnett’s and Golding’s Farms, Savage’s Station and Allen’s Farm, White Oak Swamp, and Malvern Hill.

 

Victories were won through Lee’s aggressiveness and daring in the face of McClellan’s timidity rather than by any comprehensive generalship on Lee’s part, for he was unable to exercise control over his subordinate commanders, and the individual battles could be considered tactical defeats.

Robert E. Lee

1864

War Wreaks Havoc

1864

1864 & 1865: The strain of leadership and poor health played havoc with Davis's ability to be an effective president.

 

As the Confederacy’s downfall approached many critics pointed to Jefferson Davis as one of the causes. He was defamed as “no administrator” and “the worst judge of men in the world.” He was guilty of scorning reconciliation and did little to sustain public morale.

A Loss at Home

April 30th: Jefferson Davis’s son Joseph fell to his death from the rear balcony of his home. Jefferson Davis Jr. said, “I have said all the prayers I know how, but God will not wake Joe.”

1865

Broken & Beaten

1865

Post War: Davis fled Richmond in April 1865 as Union forces closed in.

 

Captured, some say in “woman’s clothing”, he was confined “in irons” for a time at Fortress Monroe, Virginia. Jefferson Davis was released in 1867 and became a martyr for the “Lost Cause.” He returned to Mississippi and penned his memoirs before dying in 1889. His American citizenship was restored in 1978.
